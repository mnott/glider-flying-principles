# 1. Earth and Atmosphere (La Terre et l'atmosphère)

**Reference:** PDF Pages 9-12

## Introduction

Understanding the Earth and its atmosphere is fundamental to aviation and glider flying. The atmosphere is not just "air" - it's a complex mixture of gases with specific properties that directly affect how aircraft fly. This chapter establishes the foundation for understanding why and how gliders can stay aloft, how air density changes with altitude, and why atmospheric conditions matter so much for flight performance.

The atmosphere is incredibly thin relative to Earth's size - if Earth were the size of an apple, the atmosphere would be thinner than the apple's skin! Yet within this thin layer, all aviation takes place, and understanding its properties is crucial for safe and efficient flight.

## 1.1 The Earth

### 1.1.1 Size and Mass

The Earth is a nearly spherical body located approximately 150 million kilometers from the Sun. Its key dimensions are:

- **Average radius**: ~6,400 km
- **Circumference**: ~40,200 km
- **Polar radius**: 6,357 km (slightly flattened at poles)
- **Equatorial radius**: 6,371 km
- **Total mass**: 6.0 × 10²⁴ kg
- **Average density**: 5,520 kg/m³

**Why this matters for aviation:** The Earth's curvature affects navigation over long distances. The **nautical mile** (NM) is defined based on Earth's dimensions: 1 NM = 1/60th of one degree of latitude on a meridian = 1.852 km.

### 1.1.2 Gravitational Acceleration

Earth's mass creates a gravitational field that acts on all masses within it. This action is what we call **weight** or the **force of gravity**.

The measurement of this gravitational field is given by the **acceleration** it creates. At our latitudes, at sea level, the acceleration of gravity has a value of:

**g = 9.8 m/s²**

**What this means:** A body in free fall (with no air resistance) would see its velocity increase by 9.8 m/s every second. After 1 second, it would be falling at 9.8 m/s; after 2 seconds, at 19.6 m/s; and so on.

**For glider pilots:** This gravitational acceleration is what pulls us downward. It's the force we must counteract with lift to stay airborne. Understanding gravity helps us calculate glide angles, sink rates, and energy management during flight.

## 1.2 The Atmosphere

### 1.2.1 Composition and Key Properties

#### Chemical Composition

The thin layer of air surrounding Earth has the following composition by volume:

- **78% Nitrogen (N₂)** - Inert gas, provides bulk
- **21% Oxygen (O₂)** - Essential for combustion and human respiration
- **1% Water vapor, Carbon dioxide (CO₂), and rare gases** - Variable components

**Safety Note:** At high altitudes, the reduced oxygen concentration can lead to hypoxia. Glider pilots flying above 10,000 ft (3,000 m) should be aware of reduced oxygen availability.

#### Temperature

Temperature measures the **agitation of molecules** in a gas - higher temperature means molecules move faster.

**Standard temperature** at sea level: **15°C**

Temperature **decreases linearly** with altitude up to the tropopause (~10 km):
- Average decrease: **0.65°C per 100 m** (or 2°C per 1,000 ft)

**Why this matters:**
- Cold air is denser, providing better lift
- Temperature inversions (where temperature increases with altitude) can trap thermals
- Engine performance varies with temperature
- Air density calculations depend on temperature

#### Pressure

Atmospheric pressure is the effect of all molecular collisions against a surface. It's defined as:

**Pressure = Force / Surface Area** → **p = F/S**

**Units:**
- Newton per square meter (N/m²) = Pascal (Pa)
- In meteorology: hectopascal (hPa) = 100 Pa
- Old unit: millibar (mb), where 1 mb = 1 hPa

**Standard atmospheric pressure** at sea level: **1013.25 hPa**

This can also be expressed as:
- 760 mm of mercury (Hg)
- 29.92 inches Hg

**Measurement devices:**

1. **Mercury barometer**: A column of mercury whose height balances atmospheric pressure
2. **Aneroid barometer**: Uses sealed capsules that expand/contract with pressure changes - this is what's used in aircraft altimeters

**Pressure variation with altitude:**
Atmospheric pressure **decreases exponentially** with altitude. Each time you climb 5,500 m, the pressure is reduced by half:
- Sea level: 1013 hPa
- 5,500 m: ~506 hPa
- 11,000 m: ~253 hPa

**For pilots:** Altimeters work by measuring pressure. Understanding pressure changes is essential for proper altimeter setting (QNH, QFE, QNE) and altitude awareness.

#### Air Density (Mass per Volume)

Density (ρ, pronounced "rho") is the ratio between the quantity of matter and the space it occupies:

**ρ = mass/volume = m/V**

**Units:** kg/m³

**Examples:**
- Water: 1,000 kg/m³
- Mercury: 13,600 kg/m³
- Air at standard conditions: **1.2 kg/m³** (or 1.225 kg/m³ precisely)

**Note on terminology:** Anglo-Saxons use "density" for what we call mass per volume. True density (in physics) is the ratio of a substance's mass per volume to water's mass per volume - it's dimensionless. Mercury has a density of 13.6 (no units).

**The Ideal Gas Law** allows us to calculate air density from temperature and pressure:

**ρ = p / (R·T)**

Where:
- p = pressure in Pascals (Pa)
- R = gas constant = 287 J/(kg·K)
- T = temperature in Kelvin (K), where T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15

**Air density variation:**
In the lower atmosphere, density decreases with altitude such that it's **halved approximately every 6,600 meters**:
- Sea level: 1.225 kg/m³
- 6,600 m: ~0.61 kg/m³
- 13,200 m: ~0.30 kg/m³

**Critical for aviation:**
- **Lift** is proportional to air density: less dense air = less lift
- **Engine power** decreases with density: less dense air = less power
- **Propeller efficiency** changes with density
- **True airspeed** differs from indicated airspeed due to density changes

### 1.2.2 Standard Atmosphere (ICAO)

To have a reference, aeronautics defines a **standardized atmosphere** - the **ICAO Standard Atmosphere**:

**At sea level (altitude 0):**
- Temperature: 15°C (288.15 K)
- Pressure: 1013.25 hPa
- Density: 1.225 kg/m³

**Temperature decrease:** Linear at 0.65°C/100m up to the tropopause (~11 km)

**At the tropopause (~11 km):**
- Temperature: approximately -56.5°C
- Above this, temperature remains constant in the stratosphere

**Why we need a standard atmosphere:**
1. **Altimeter calibration**: All altimeters are calibrated to standard atmosphere
2. **Performance calculations**: Aircraft performance charts reference standard conditions
3. **Common reference**: Allows pilots worldwide to communicate using same baseline
4. **Flight planning**: Helps calculate true airspeed, density altitude, and performance

**Practical application:** When you set your altimeter to 1013.25 hPa (standard setting, called QNE), it shows your pressure altitude - your altitude in the standard atmosphere model.

## Questions / Fragen / Questions

### Q1: What is the approximate circumference of Earth?

**A:** Approximately 40,200 km. This is important for navigation calculations, and the nautical mile is defined based on this dimension (1 NM = 1.852 km = 1/60th of one degree of latitude).

**DE:** Ungefähr 40.200 km. Dies ist wichtig für Navigationsberechnungen, und die Seemeile wird basierend auf dieser Dimension definiert.

**FR:** Environ 40.200 km. C'est important pour les calculs de navigation, et le mile nautique est défini sur la base de cette dimension.

### Q2: What is the value of gravitational acceleration at sea level?

**A:** 9.8 m/s². This means that in free fall (without air resistance), an object's velocity increases by 9.8 meters per second every second.

**DE:** 9,8 m/s². Das bedeutet, dass im freien Fall (ohne Luftwiderstand) die Geschwindigkeit eines Objekts jede Sekunde um 9,8 Meter pro Sekunde zunimmt.

**FR:** 9,8 m/s². Cela signifie qu'en chute libre (sans résistance de l'air), la vitesse d'un objet augmente de 9,8 mètres par seconde chaque seconde.

---

### Q3: What is the chemical composition of the atmosphere?

**A:** The atmosphere is composed of approximately 78% Nitrogen (N₂), 21% Oxygen (O₂), and 1% water vapor, carbon dioxide (CO₂), and rare gases.

**DE:** Die Atmosphäre besteht aus ungefähr 78% Stickstoff (N₂), 21% Sauerstoff (O₂) und 1% Wasserdampf, Kohlendioxid (CO₂) und Edelgasen.

**FR:** L'atmosphère est composée d'environ 78% d'azote (N₂), 21% d'oxygène (O₂) et 1% de vapeur d'eau, de dioxyde de carbone (CO₂) et de gaz rares.

---

### Q4: How does temperature change with altitude in the standard atmosphere?

**A:** Temperature decreases linearly with altitude at an average rate of 0.65°C per 100 meters (or 2°C per 1,000 feet) up to the tropopause at approximately 10 km altitude.

**DE:** Die Temperatur nimmt mit der Höhe linear ab, mit einer durchschnittlichen Rate von 0,65°C pro 100 Meter (oder 2°C pro 1.000 Fuß) bis zur Tropopause in etwa 10 km Höhe.

**FR:** La température diminue linéairement avec l'altitude à un taux moyen de 0,65°C par 100 mètres (ou 2°C par 1.000 pieds) jusqu'à la tropopause à environ 10 km d'altitude.

---

### Q5: What is standard atmospheric pressure at sea level?

**A:** Standard atmospheric pressure at sea level is 1013.25 hPa (hectopascals), which equals 760 mm Hg or 29.92 inches Hg. This is the reference pressure used for altimeter calibration.

**DE:** Der Standardluftdruck auf Meereshöhe beträgt 1013,25 hPa (Hektopascal), was 760 mm Hg oder 29,92 Zoll Hg entspricht. Dies ist der Referenzdruck für die Höhenmesser-Kalibrierung.

**FR:** La pression atmosphérique standard au niveau de la mer est de 1013,25 hPa (hectopascals), ce qui équivaut à 760 mm Hg ou 29,92 pouces Hg. C'est la pression de référence utilisée pour l'étalonnage de l'altimètre.

---

### Q6: How does atmospheric pressure change with altitude?

**A:** Atmospheric pressure decreases exponentially with altitude. Each time you ascend 5,500 meters, the pressure is reduced by half. This is why altimeters (which measure pressure) can be used to determine altitude.

**DE:** Der Luftdruck nimmt exponentiell mit der Höhe ab. Jedes Mal, wenn man 5.500 Meter aufsteigt, wird der Druck halbiert. Deshalb können Höhenmesser (die den Druck messen) zur Höhenbestimmung verwendet werden.

**FR:** La pression atmosphérique décroît exponentiellement avec l'altitude. Chaque fois que l'on s'élève de 5.500 mètres, la pression est réduite de moitié. C'est pourquoi les altimètres (qui mesurent la pression) peuvent être utilisés pour déterminer l'altitude.

---

### Q7: What is air density at standard conditions and how does it vary with altitude?

**A:** Air density at standard sea level conditions is 1.2 kg/m³ (or 1.225 kg/m³ precisely). It decreases with altitude, being approximately halved every 6,600 meters. This affects lift generation, as lift is proportional to air density.

**DE:** Die Luftdichte bei Standard-Seehöhe beträgt 1,2 kg/m³ (oder genau 1,225 kg/m³). Sie nimmt mit der Höhe ab und halbiert sich etwa alle 6.600 Meter. Dies beeinflusst die Auftriebserzeugung, da der Auftrieb proportional zur Luftdichte ist.

**FR:** La masse volumique de l'air aux conditions standard au niveau de la mer est de 1,2 kg/m³ (ou précisément 1,225 kg/m³). Elle diminue avec l'altitude, étant approximativement divisée par deux tous les 6.600 mètres. Cela affecte la génération de portance, car la portance est proportionnelle à la masse volumique de l'air.

---

### Q8 (Multiple Choice): At what altitude is atmospheric pressure approximately half of sea level pressure?

- A) 2,500 m
- B) 5,500 m
- C) 10,000 m
- D) 15,000 m

**A:** B) 5,500 m. Atmospheric pressure decreases exponentially, and it's reduced by approximately half every 5,500 meters of altitude gain.

**DE:** B) 5.500 m. Der Luftdruck nimmt exponentiell ab und wird etwa alle 5.500 Meter Höhengewinn halbiert.

**FR:** B) 5.500 m. La pression atmosphérique décroît exponentiellement et est réduite d'environ la moitié tous les 5.500 mètres de gain d'altitude.
