# 5. Stalls and Spins (Décrochages et Vrille)

**Reference:** PDF Pages 51-54 (Part 2)

## Introduction

Understanding stalls and spins is absolutely critical for every glider pilot's safety. These aerodynamic phenomena occur when the wing exceeds its critical angle of attack, resulting in a loss of lift that can lead to dangerous flight conditions. While a properly executed stall is a controlled maneuver used in training, an inadvertent stall—especially near the ground—can be fatal. Spins, which result from asymmetric stalls, add rotational complexity that requires specific recovery procedures.

This lesson explores why stalls occur, how to recognize their warning signs, different types of stalls, and the physics behind spins. Understanding these concepts isn't just academic—it's about developing the instinctive recognition and response skills that can save your life.

**Why this matters:** Most fatal glider accidents occur at low altitude during takeoff, approach, or landing phases when stalls and spins can develop without sufficient height for recovery. Recognizing the conditions that lead to stalls and knowing immediate recovery actions is essential airmanship.

## Flight at High Angles of Attack

### The Critical Angle of Attack

In earlier chapters, we learned how airflow around a wing creates the aerodynamic resultant force (lift). We also learned that angle of attack can be increased up to a **critical angle of attack** (angle d'attaque critique), beyond which the airflow can no longer follow the wing's upper surface curvature. This results in flow separation, causing a sudden and significant loss of lift.

**What actually happens:** As angle of attack increases, the pressure differential between upper and lower wing surfaces grows, creating more lift. However, at the critical angle (typically 15-20° depending on airfoil design), the airflow separates from the upper surface, creating turbulent wake. The smooth laminar flow breaks down, pressure equalize more rapidly, and lift drops dramatically—often by 30-50%.

### When Do We Approach the Critical Angle?

We approach the critical angle of attack when flying slowly and nose-high, in the left portion of the speed polar (between the polar's top point and stall speed). Common situations include:

- **Thermalling:** Circling tightly in rising air at slow speeds
- **Slow turns:** Attempting to turn with insufficient airspeed
- **Landing approach:** Trying to "stretch" a glide with excessive nose-up attitude

**Warning signs** on most gliders include:
- **Mushy controls:** Reduced control surface effectiveness
- **Buffeting/vibrations:** Turbulent airflow creating airframe vibrations
- **Reduced airflow noise:** The characteristic "whoosh" diminishes

These warnings vary by glider type—modern sailplanes with laminar flow wings may give less warning than older designs with turbulent flow characteristics.

### Understanding the Speed Polar Context

The speed polar diagram plots sink rate against airspeed. The left side of the polar (slow flight region) shows:
- **Minimum sink speed:** The most efficient thermal climbing speed
- **Stall speed:** The absolute minimum controllable airspeed
- The region between these points is where stall awareness is critical

Flying on this part of the polar requires constant attention to angle of attack through airspeed monitoring and control feel.

## Types of Stalls

### 1. Nose-Up Stall (Gentle Stall)

**Scenario:** Starting from a slightly nose-up attitude, the stall occurs gently.

**What happens:**
1. As critical angle is exceeded, the glider pitches nose-down in a small diving motion (abattée)
2. The pilot releases back pressure on the stick ("rendre la main")
3. The glider returns to normal flight attitude through a gentle recovery (légère ressource)

**Why it's gentle:** The wing typically stalls progressively from root to tip due to:
- **Aerodynamic twist (washout):** Wing tips have lower angle of incidence
- **Geometric twist:** Physical twist built into wing design
- **Stall strips:** Devices at wing root that trigger early stall warning

This progressive stall design ensures:
- Ailerons (at wing tips) remain in clean airflow and stay effective
- Control authority maintained throughout stall
- Predictable, controllable stall behavior

**Recovery technique:**
1. **Release back pressure** (push stick forward slightly)
2. Allow nose to drop naturally
3. Gentle recovery once airspeed increases

### 2. Steep Nose-Up Stall

**Scenario:** The aircraft is strongly pitched up at stall onset.

**What happens:**
- The nose drops more sharply and pronouncedly (abattée plus sèche et prononcée)
- Any slight asymmetry can trigger a spin entry
- More altitude is lost during recovery

**The danger:** In a steep attitude, the wings have very high angle of attack. When they stall, the nose drop is more violent, and if one wing stalls before the other (due to turbulence, uncoordinated controls, or aircraft roll), an incipient spin can develop instantly.

**Where this occurs:**
- Aggressive pull-ups from dives
- Attempting to climb too steeply during winch launch
- Panic pull-up when terrain appears close

### 3. Dynamic Stall (Décrochage Dynamique)

Dynamic stall is particularly dangerous because it occurs at speeds well above the polar's normal stall speed.

**How it happens:**

**A. Excessive G-loading (Abrupt Pull-up)**
- Load factor increases during sharp pull-ups
- When load factor >1, apparent weight increases
- Stall speed increases proportionally: V_stall_new = V_stall × √n (where n = load factor)
- Even though flying well above normal stall speed, the wing can exceed critical angle

**Example:** At 2G (steep turn or pullout), stall speed increases by 41%. A glider with 45 kt stall speed would stall at 64 kt in a 2G maneuver.

**B. Vertical Gust Encounter**
- Strong vertical updraft suddenly changes effective angle of attack
- Gust velocity adds vectorially to aircraft's velocity
- Resulting angle of attack can exceed critical value
- Wing stalls despite adequate indicated airspeed

**Why it's dangerous:**
- **Unexpected:** Occurs at "safe" airspeeds
- **Sudden:** No gradual warning
- **Spin risk:** Can trigger immediate spin entry
- **Low altitude:** Often occurs during low-level maneuvering or in turbulent conditions near terrain

**Prevention:**
- Respect maneuvering speed (VA) limits
- Increase approach speeds in turbulent conditions
- Avoid aggressive control inputs at low airspeed
- Understand relationship between G-loading and stall speed

## Spins (La Vrille / Autorotation)

### What is a Spin?

A spin is a stabilized rotating condition where:
- Both wings are stalled
- Each wing has a very high but different angle of attack
- Rotation can be rapid: approximately **2 seconds per turn**
- Altitude loss: approximately **100 meters per turn** (varies by aircraft)

**The physics:** In a spin, the glider descends in a helical path. The inner wing has a higher angle of attack and less lift than the outer wing, but both are stalled. The aircraft rotates around a vertical axis while descending steeply.

### What Causes Spin Entry?

A spin requires two conditions:
1. **Stalled wing**
2. **Asymmetry** (yaw)

**Common triggers:**
- **Rudder input at stall:** Stepping on rudder as wing stalls
- **Skidding slow turn:** Uncoordinated turn at low airspeed
- **Turbulence:** Strong turbulence during slow flight causes asymmetric flow disruption
- **Cross-control:** Opposite aileron and rudder inputs near stall

**Example scenario:** A pilot enters a turn at low altitude (e.g., turning from base to final approach). Flying too slowly, the pilot tries to tighten the turn with aileron alone. The down-going aileron increases drag on that wing (adverse yaw), causing the nose to swing opposite to the turn direction. The pilot adds rudder to bring the nose around. The inner wing, already at high angle of attack due to slow speed, exceeds critical angle first. The aircraft snap-rolls and enters a spin.

### Spin Recovery Procedure

The standard recovery sequence:

1. **Full opposite rudder** - Stops the rotation
2. **Pause** - Allow rotation to stop
3. **Release back pressure** (push stick slightly forward) - Reduces angle of attack below critical
4. **Neutral rudder** - Once rotation stops
5. **Gentle recovery** - Pull out of resulting dive carefully

**Why in this order?**
- Rudder is most effective at stopping rotation
- Reducing angle of attack is essential to unstall the wings
- Premature pull-up can restart the spin or cause accelerated stall

### Special Spin Conditions

**Flat Spin:**
The most dangerous spin condition. In a flat spin:
- Aircraft attitude is nearly horizontal
- Rotation rate may be very high
- Tail surfaces may be in turbulent wake from fuselage
- **Rudder effectiveness is severely reduced** (effet de masque aérodynamique)
- Recovery may be very difficult or impossible

**Why flat spins are dangerous:** The horizontal attitude means the elevator and rudder are in disturbed airflow behind the fuselage and wings. Normal recovery controls may be ineffective. Some aircraft cannot be recovered from flat spins—which is why spin testing is a critical part of certification.

**Prevention is critical:** Avoid pro-spin control inputs (continued back stick and rudder in direction of spin).

## Stall Prevention Devices

Modern gliders incorporate several design features to improve stall behavior:

### 1. Stall Strips (Barrettes de Décrochage)

Small wedge-shaped strips attached to the wing's leading edge near the root. They:
- Deliberately disrupt airflow at the wing root first
- Ensure root stalls before tips
- Maintain aileron effectiveness during stall
- Provide buffet warning to pilot

### 2. Leading Edge Apex (Apex de Bord d'Attaque)

A sharp leading edge section that:
- Creates controlled flow separation
- Provides progressive stall characteristics
- Gives better stall warning

### 3. Vortex Generators (Générateurs de Tourbillons)

Small vanes on the upper wing surface that:
- Create small vortices that energize boundary layer
- Delay flow separation
- Extend range of usable angles of attack
- Improve control effectiveness near stall

These devices give the aircraft "healthy behavior" during approach to stall and during the break itself—keeping the aircraft controllable and giving the pilot adequate warning and authority to recover.

## Questions / Fragen / Questions

### Q1: What is the critical angle of attack and what happens when it's exceeded?

**A:** The critical angle of attack is the angle between the wing chord line and relative airflow at which the airflow can no longer follow the wing's upper surface curvature. When exceeded, flow separation occurs, causing a sudden and significant loss of lift—this is a stall. The critical angle is typically 15-20° depending on airfoil design.

**DE:** Der kritische Anstellwinkel ist der Winkel zwischen der Flügelprofilsehne und der relativen Luftströmung, bei dem die Luftströmung der Krümmung der Flügeloberseite nicht mehr folgen kann. Wird dieser überschritten, kommt es zur Strömungsablösung, die einen plötzlichen und erheblichen Auftriebsverlust verursacht – das ist ein Strömungsabriss. Der kritische Winkel liegt typischerweise bei 15-20°, abhängig vom Flügelprofil.

**FR:** L'angle d'attaque critique est l'angle entre la corde du profil de l'aile et l'écoulement relatif, au-delà duquel l'écoulement d'air ne peut plus suivre la courbure de l'extrados. Lorsqu'il est dépassé, un décollement de l'écoulement se produit, provoquant une perte soudaine et importante de portance – c'est le décrochage. L'angle critique se situe typiquement entre 15 et 20° selon le profil d'aile.

### Q2: What are the three main warning signs of an approaching stall in most gliders?

**A:** The three main warning signs are: (1) **Mushy controls** - reduced effectiveness of control surfaces, (2) **Buffeting or vibrations** - caused by turbulent airflow creating airframe shaking, and (3) **Reduced airflow noise** - the characteristic aerodynamic sound diminishes as airflow becomes less organized.

**DE:** Die drei Hauptwarnzeichen sind: (1) **Schwammige Steuerung** - verringerte Wirksamkeit der Steuerflächen, (2) **Rütteln oder Vibrationen** - verursacht durch turbulente Luftströmung, die das Flugzeug schüttelt, und (3) **Vermindertes Strömungsgeräusch** - das charakteristische aerodynamische Geräusch nimmt ab, wenn die Luftströmung weniger organisiert wird.

**FR:** Les trois principaux signes d'alerte sont : (1) **Commandes molles** - efficacité réduite des surfaces de commande, (2) **Vibrations ou secousses** - causées par l'écoulement turbulent créant des vibrations de la cellule, et (3) **Diminution du bruit d'écoulement** - le son aérodynamique caractéristique diminue lorsque l'écoulement devient moins organisé.

### Q3: Why do glider wings typically stall from root to tip?

**A:** Glider wings are designed to stall progressively from root to tip through aerodynamic twist (washout) or geometric twist. This ensures that when the root stalls first, the ailerons at the wing tips remain in clean, unstalled airflow and stay effective. This gives the pilot continued roll control during the stall, making the aircraft safer and more controllable. Stall strips and other devices at the wing root reinforce this behavior.

**DE:** Segelflugzeugflügel sind so konstruiert, dass sie progressiv von der Wurzel zur Spitze abreißen, durch aerodynamische Verwindung (Washout) oder geometrische Verdrehung. Dadurch wird sichergestellt, dass bei einem Abriss zuerst an der Wurzel die Querruder an den Flügelspitzen in sauberer, nicht abgerissener Strömung bleiben und wirksam bleiben. Dies gibt dem Piloten während des Strömungsabrisses weiterhin Rollkontrolle, was das Flugzeug sicherer und kontrollierbarer macht. Abreißleisten und andere Vorrichtungen an der Flügelwurzel verstärken dieses Verhalten.

**FR:** Les ailes de planeur sont conçues pour décrocher progressivement de l'emplanture vers l'extrémité grâce à une torsion aérodynamique (washout) ou géométrique. Cela garantit que lorsque l'emplanture décroche en premier, les ailerons aux extrémités des ailes restent dans un écoulement propre et non décroché, restant ainsi efficaces. Cela donne au pilote un contrôle en roulis continu pendant le décrochage, rendant l'appareil plus sûr et plus contrôlable. Les barrettes de décrochage et autres dispositifs à l'emplanture renforcent ce comportement.

### Q4: What is a dynamic stall and why is it particularly dangerous?

**A:** A dynamic stall occurs when the wing stalls at an airspeed significantly above the normal stall speed shown on the speed polar. It happens in two situations: (1) during aggressive pull-ups where G-loading increases apparent weight and stall speed, or (2) when encountering strong vertical gusts that suddenly increase the effective angle of attack. It's particularly dangerous because it's unexpected—occurring at "safe" airspeeds—and can trigger immediate spin entry without warning.

**DE:** Ein dynamischer Strömungsabriss tritt auf, wenn der Flügel bei einer Geschwindigkeit deutlich über der normalen Überziehgeschwindigkeit abreißt, die in der Geschwindigkeitspolare angegeben ist. Er tritt in zwei Situationen auf: (1) bei aggressiven Hochziehmanövern, bei denen die G-Belastung das scheinbare Gewicht und die Überziehgeschwindigkeit erhöht, oder (2) bei starken vertikalen Böen, die plötzlich den effektiven Anstellwinkel erhöhen. Er ist besonders gefährlich, weil er unerwartet auftritt – bei "sicheren" Geschwindigkeiten – und sofort einen Trudeleingang ohne Vorwarnung auslösen kann.

**FR:** Un décrochage dynamique se produit lorsque l'aile décroche à une vitesse nettement supérieure à la vitesse de décrochage normale indiquée sur la polaire des vitesses. Il se produit dans deux situations : (1) lors de ressources brutales où le facteur de charge augmente le poids apparent et la vitesse de décrochage, ou (2) lors de la rencontre de rafales verticales fortes qui augmentent soudainement l'angle d'attaque effectif. Il est particulièrement dangereux car il est inattendu – se produisant à des vitesses "sûres" – et peut déclencher une entrée en vrille immédiate sans avertissement.

### Q5: What is a spin and what conditions are required for it to develop?

**A:** A spin (vrille or autorotation) is a stabilized rotating condition where both wings are stalled with very high but different angles of attack. The rotation can be rapid (about 2 seconds per turn) with significant altitude loss (approximately 100 meters per turn). Two conditions are required: (1) a stalled wing, and (2) asymmetry (yaw) caused by rudder input, skidding turn, turbulence, or cross-control inputs.

**DE:** Ein Trudeln (vrille oder Autorotation) ist ein stabilisierter rotierender Zustand, bei dem beide Flügel mit sehr hohen, aber unterschiedlichen Anstellwinkeln abgerissen sind. Die Rotation kann schnell sein (etwa 2 Sekunden pro Umdrehung) mit erheblichem Höhenverlust (etwa 100 Meter pro Umdrehung). Zwei Bedingungen sind erforderlich: (1) ein abgerissener Flügel und (2) Asymmetrie (Gieren), verursacht durch Seitenrudereingabe, schleudernde Kurve, Turbulenz oder gekreuzte Steuereingaben.

**FR:** Une vrille (ou autorotation) est une situation de rotation stabilisée dans laquelle les deux ailes sont décrochées avec des angles d'attaque très grands mais différents. La rotation peut être rapide (environ 2 secondes par tour) avec une perte d'altitude importante (environ 100 mètres par tour). Deux conditions sont nécessaires : (1) une aile décrochée, et (2) une asymétrie (lacet) causée par une action du palonnier, un virage dérapé, de la turbulence ou des commandes croisées.

### Q6: What is the correct spin recovery procedure?

**A:** The standard spin recovery sequence is:
1. **Full opposite rudder** - to stop the rotation
2. **Pause** - allow rotation to cease
3. **Release back pressure** (push stick slightly forward) - to reduce angle of attack below critical
4. **Neutral rudder** - once rotation stops
5. **Gentle recovery** - pull out carefully from the resulting dive

Apply opposite rudder first because it's most effective at stopping rotation. The stick must be eased forward to unstall the wings. Premature pull-up can restart the spin.

**DE:** Die Standard-Trudelabfangsequenz ist:
1. **Volles Gegenruder** - um die Rotation zu stoppen
2. **Pause** - Rotation aufhören lassen
3. **Rückdruck lösen** (Steuerknüppel leicht nach vorne drücken) - um Anstellwinkel unter kritischen Wert zu reduzieren
4. **Neutrales Seitenruder** - sobald Rotation stoppt
5. **Sanftes Abfangen** - vorsichtig aus dem resultierenden Sturzflug abfangen

Zuerst Gegenruder anwenden, da dies am effektivsten die Rotation stoppt. Der Steuerknüppel muss nach vorne bewegt werden, um die Flügel wieder in den Normalflug zu bringen. Verfrühtes Hochziehen kann das Trudeln neu starten.

**FR:** La séquence standard de sortie de vrille est :
1. **Palonnier à fond dans le sens opposé** - pour arrêter la rotation
2. **Pause** - laisser la rotation cesser
3. **Relâcher la pression arrière** (pousser légèrement le manche vers l'avant) - pour réduire l'angle d'attaque en dessous du critique
4. **Palonnier neutre** - une fois la rotation arrêtée
5. **Ressource douce** - sortir prudemment du piqué résultant

Appliquer d'abord le palonnier opposé car c'est le plus efficace pour arrêter la rotation. Le manche doit être poussé vers l'avant pour désaborder les ailes. Une ressource prématurée peut relancer la vrille.

### Q7: Multiple Choice - Which of the following is TRUE about flat spins?

A) They are easier to recover from than normal spins
B) The rudder becomes more effective due to increased airflow
C) The tail surfaces are in wake from the fuselage, making rudder less effective
D) They only occur in aerobatic aircraft

**A:** **C is correct.** In a flat spin, the aircraft is in a nearly horizontal attitude with high rotation rate. The elevator and rudder are in the turbulent wake (sillage) from the fuselage, creating an aerodynamic masking effect (effet de masque aérodynamique). This makes the rudder severely less effective or even ineffective, making recovery very difficult or impossible. Prevention through proper control use is critical.

**DE:** **C ist richtig.** Bei einem flachen Trudeln befindet sich das Flugzeug in einer nahezu horizontalen Lage mit hoher Rotationsrate. Das Höhen- und Seitenruder befinden sich im turbulenten Nachlauf (Sillage) des Rumpfes, was einen aerodynamischen Maskierungseffekt (effet de masque aérodynamique) erzeugt. Dies macht das Seitenruder stark weniger wirksam oder sogar unwirksam, wodurch die Wiederherstellung sehr schwierig oder unmöglich wird. Vorbeugung durch richtige Steuerungsnutzung ist entscheidend.

**FR:** **C est correct.** Dans une vrille plate, l'appareil est dans une attitude presque horizontale avec un taux de rotation élevé. La profondeur et la dérive sont dans le sillage turbulent du fuselage, créant un effet de masque aérodynamique. Cela rend la gouverne de direction beaucoup moins efficace ou même inefficace, rendant la sortie très difficile ou impossible. La prévention par une utilisation appropriée des commandes est critique.

## Summary / Zusammenfassung / Résumé

**EN:** Stalls occur when the critical angle of attack is exceeded, causing flow separation and sudden lift loss. Warning signs include mushy controls, buffeting, and reduced noise. Recovery requires releasing back pressure to reduce angle of attack. Dynamic stalls are particularly dangerous, occurring above normal stall speed due to G-loading or gusts. Spins develop when asymmetry (yaw) combines with a stalled wing, creating autorotation. Recovery requires full opposite rudder to stop rotation, then forward stick to reduce angle of attack. Flat spins, with reduced control effectiveness, may be unrecoverable. Modern gliders use stall strips, wing twist, and other devices to ensure progressive root-first stalling, maintaining aileron effectiveness and controllability. Understanding these phenomena and their prevention is essential for safe flight operations.

Warnzeichen sind schwammige Steuerung, Rütteln und vermindertes Geräusch. Die Wiederherstellung erfordert das Lösen des Rückdrucks zur Reduzierung des Anstellwinkels. Dynamische Strömungsabrisse sind besonders gefährlich und treten über der normalen Überziehgeschwindigkeit aufgrund von G-Belastung oder Böen auf. Trudeln entwickelt sich, wenn Asymmetrie (Gieren) mit einem abgerissenen Flügel kombiniert wird und Autorotation erzeugt. Die Wiederherstellung erfordert volles Gegenruder zum Stoppen der Rotation, dann Vorwärtsbewegung des Steuerknüppels zur Reduzierung des Anstellwinkels.
